Abstract

Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on exemplary practices from the Swiss federal administration's EMBAG legislation, Germany's OZG reform programme, ZenDiS and the Sovereign Cloud Stack initiative, and the wider European sovereign technology community — including the GovStack building-block framework, CONSUL Democracy, and the eVaka shared-resource model — we derive a first-principles implementation roadmap, stakeholder engagement framework, and procurement strategy applicable to municipalities of all sizes.

We evaluate the core technology components — identity management, document management, workflow automation, citizen participation, secure communications, open data publication, geographic information systems, public websites, and cloud infrastructure — against criteria of digital sovereignty, interoperability, total cost of ownership, and civic alignment. We present empirical total cost of ownership evidence: the French Gendarmerie's GendBuntu project achieved a 40% TCO reduction and €2 million in annual licence savings across 103,164 workstations; the City of Toulouse documented €1.8 million in savings over three years. We conclude that open-source municipal technology stacks are not merely technically viable but fiscally superior, democratically necessary, legally mandated in an increasing number of jurisdictions, and now supported by a mature institutional ecosystem including ZenDiS, govdigital eG, and the GovStack initiative. The paper provides a phased 36-month implementation roadmap with a small-municipality track and concrete procurement guidance.

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; and fiscal pressures require sustainable, cost-effective technology investments. Yet the majority of city governments worldwide remain trapped in a cycle of proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede rather than enable good governance [1, 29].

The consequences of this dependency are well-documented: vendor lock-in raises switching costs and bargaining asymmetry [4]; proprietary formats obstruct cross-agency data exchange and transparency [45]; closed-source infrastructure prevents independent security auditing [26]; and recurring licence fees drain budgets that could otherwise fund service delivery [3, 5]. Most fundamentally, when the software running democratic institutions is owned and controlled by private actors, a structural conflict of interest emerges between the public interest and corporate imperatives [4]. The foundational argument, articulated most clearly by Lathrop and Ruma in Open Government (2010), is that the data generated by democratic processes and the software that governs those processes should, as a default, be available to the public that pays for them [60].

A different path is both possible and proven. Switzerland's 2023 EMBAG legislation mandates that federal software developed with public funds be released as open source by default [1]. Germany's OZG reform programme, ZenDiS and its OpenDesk initiative, the Sovereign Cloud Stack, and the openCode.de platform represent the most ambitious coordinated open-source government technology programme in Europe [2, 3, 42, 47]. This paper synthesises these developments into a practical strategy for municipal governments.

1.1 Scope and Definitions

Municipal government refers to city and local government authorities, including Gemeinden, Kommunen, Städte, and equivalent structures in Swiss cantons. The strategy scales from small municipalities (population 5,000–50,000) to large cities (population 500,000+), with modulation in Section 5.6.

Digital sovereignty is the ability of a government to make independent decisions about its digital infrastructure, including the right to inspect, modify, audit, and migrate software without dependency on a single vendor [3, 59].

1.2 Research Questions

  1. What does a state-of-the-art open-source municipal technology stack look like in 2026?
  2. What lessons can be drawn from the Swiss, German, and European sovereign technology communities?
  3. What is the optimal phased implementation pathway for city governments of varying sizes?
  4. How should procurement, stakeholder engagement, and risk management be structured to maximise success probability?

2. Methodology

This paper employs a multi-method research design combining systematic literature review, comparative policy analysis, technology stack evaluation using a structured scoring matrix, stakeholder analysis, and total cost of ownership analysis drawing on empirical case studies from France, Italy, and Czech Republic.

Comparative policy analysis covers Switzerland (EMBAG 2023 [1], E-Government Strategy 2024–2027 [16], Digital Switzerland Strategy 2025 [61], e-ID Act 2024 [56]), Germany (OZG 2017/2024 [2], ZenDiS mandate [47], openCode.de [10]), and the European Union (Interoperable Europe Act 2024 [6], EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 [5], EU Data Act 2023 [51], Data Governance Act 2022 [52]).

2.1 Inclusion Criteria

Sources were included if they: (a) address open-source software in public administration; (b) cover governmental digital transformation strategy; (c) relate to the European, Swiss, or German regulatory context; or (d) provide primary data on municipal technology implementations.

2.2 Limitations

This paper is a citation-complete draft (v0.2.0). Four citations remain partially verified against primary sources; these are noted in the source registry (Mem/source-registry.md). Technology stack assessments reflect publicly available information as of June 2026.

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations of E-Government

The academic literature on e-government has evolved through four broad phases [30]. First-generation e-government (1995–2005) focused on digitising processes. Second-generation (2005–2012) emphasised online service delivery. Third-generation (2012–2020) introduced open data and participatory platforms. The current fourth generation (2020–present) is characterised by platform government, digital identity infrastructure, and the sovereignty turn [45].

Wirtz and Weyerer's holistic e-government maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7]. The EPRS 2023 study on digital sovereignty [59] explicitly endorses the Sovereign Cloud Stack approach as a model for EU public administration.

3.2 Digital Sovereignty in Public Administration

The EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 established "sharing and reuse" as a core principle [5]. The 2024 Interoperable Europe Act creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. Germany's Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) provides a fully open-source cloud platform (OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph) [3, 11]. The govdigital eG cooperative offers SCS-based infrastructure under framework agreements satisfying German procurement law [23].

Switzerland's EMBAG, in force 1 January 2024, requires all federal software developed with public funds to be released as open source [1]. The 2025 Digital Switzerland Strategy [61] reinforces this with specific emphasis on the e-ID as foundational digital infrastructure.

The EU Data Act (Regulation EU 2023/2854), applying from September 2025, extends digital sovereignty obligations to smart city infrastructure [51]. The Data Governance Act (Regulation EU 2022/868), applying from September 2023, enables municipalities to register as data altruism organisations [52].

3.3 The German OZG Ecosystem

The OZG 2.0 reform (2024) introduces "Once Only," "Einer für Alle" (EfA), BundID, and FITKO [2, 9]. As of early 2025: the federal government had digitised its 115 prioritised services, but only 196 of 575 state-level services (34%) were available nationwide [62]. BundID had ~6 million accounts by March 2025 — 7.5% of eligible citizens.

ZenDiS GmbH [47], founded December 2022 by BMI, manages OpenDesk (launched end of 2024 as SaaS: Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element + Collabora) and openCode.de (300+ repositories) [42, 10]. Fraunhofer IESE's 2024 research confirms: barriers to OSS adoption are organisational, not technical [58].

3.4 Swiss Cantonal and Federal Digital Services

Key Swiss digital infrastructure: Fedlex, opendata.swiss (CKAN-based), and GEVER. The eCH Association [49] provides interoperability standards equivalent to Germany's XÖV:

  • eCH-0007: Historicised municipal directory
  • eCH-0010: Postal address format
  • eCH-0044: Person identification
  • eCH-0058: Delivery interface (secure document delivery between authorities)
  • eCH-0211: Building application (municipality–canton data exchange)

The Swiss e-ID (BGEID) [56]: passed by Parliament on 20 December 2024; approved in referendum 28 September 2025 (50.39% yes); state-managed with no central data storage; CHF 182 million budget for 2023–2028; rollout summer 2026.

3.5 Open-Source Communities and Sovereign Technology

Decidim [12]: 400+ deployments, strong German/French/Italian support. CONSUL Democracy [50]: 250+ deployments, 45 languages, 100M+ citizens; CONSUL Democracy Foundation (est. 2019) ensures maintenance. EU OSOR case study published [55]. Nextcloud [13]: most widely deployed OSS collaboration platform in European public administration. OpenDesk [42]: launched end 2024 by ZenDiS as curated SaaS. GovStack [48] (ITU/DIAL/BMZ/Estonia): 9 building-block specifications for interoperable digital government; deployed in Montenegro (December 2024, 25 local self-governments), Rwanda, Kenya, Ethiopia.

3.6 Total Cost of Ownership Evidence

French Gendarmerie GendBuntu [53]: 103,164 workstations on Linux + LibreOffice by June 2024. ~40% TCO reduction; ~€2 million annual licence savings. Largest documented government open-source desktop migration.

City of Toulouse [54]: €1.8 million savings over three years; 90% desktop migration to open-source.

French ministries [53]: 11 ministries, 500,000 LibreOffice workstations. Occitanie region launched digital independence strategy in 2023 following Microsoft contract cost escalation.

TCO summary: 30–40% cost reductions consistently documented. Cooperative procurement (govdigital eG, cantonal IT cooperatives) spreads implementation costs and improves economics for small Gemeinden.

3.7 Small Municipality Case Studies

eVaka — Espoo, Finland [57]: Open-source early education management with shared resource model across Finnish municipalities. LGPL-2.1 licensed; available on GitHub. Cityvizor — Czech Republic: Open-source municipal budget transparency platform; shared governance model. EfA for small German municipalities: One Land develops a service; all municipalities access it via openCode.de [24]. Key insight: The binding constraint is cooperative participation, not technical capability.

3.8 ZenDiS and the Institutional Architecture

ZenDiS GmbH [47] + govdigital eG [23] + Sovereign Cloud Stack [3] form an integrated institutional ecosystem: ZenDiS curates the application stack; govdigital eG provides cooperative SCS-based cloud infrastructure; openCode.de serves as shared code repository. German municipalities have a complete, sovereign digital infrastructure pathway they do not need to assemble alone. ZenDiS targeted tenders for OpenDesk marketing licences in Q1 2026, creating a structured procurement pathway for municipalities.

  2. Full-stack TCO studies (complete municipal administration stack)
  3. User experience research comparing open-source and proprietary municipal services
  4. BundesMessenger adoption data (not publicly available)

Recommended: Nextcloud [13] + OpenProject [20]. For Swiss municipalities requiring GEVER compliance, cantonal solutions provide the compliance layer; Nextcloud as collaborative front-end; file exchange uses eCH-0058 [49]. Scores: licence 5 (AGPL-3.0), maturity 5 (400,000+ orgs), interoperability 4 (WebDAV, CalDAV, CMIS, eCH-0058), security 5 (ISO 27001), TCO 5 (no per-seat licence).

Recommended: Camunda Platform 8 Community [33] (BPMN 2.0-native, XÖV/eCH integration). Alternative: Flowable (Apache 2.0, lighter-weight). Licence score 4 (Community Apache 2.0; Enterprise proprietary), interoperability 5 (BPMN 2.0, REST, event-driven, XÖV/eCH), TCO 3 (Community free; scale may need Enterprise).

4.6 Open Data Publication

Recommended: CKAN [22] (AGPL-3.0) — powers opendata.swiss, data.gov, data.gov.uk. Supports DCAT-AP, DCAT-AP.de, OAI-PMH, SPARQL. Municipalities publishing via CKAN can contribute directly to opendata.swiss or GovData.de through automated harvesting. Data Governance Act registration [52] as data altruism organisation is compatible with DCAT-AP metadata.

4.7 Geographic Information Systems

QGIS [37] (GPL-2.0+) for spatial data editing and analysis. GeoServer for OGC-compliant spatial data publication (WMS, WFS, WCS; INSPIRE Directive compliant). OpenStreetMap [36] as base cartographic layer. Switzerland's swisstopo and Germany's BKG provide open governmental base map data under OGD licences.

4.8 Public Website and Content Management

TYPO3 [19]: dominant in Swiss and German public administration; TYPO3 Association provides long-term support and public-sector extensions. Drupal: strong international track record (European Commission). Both support WCAG 2.1 AA / BITV 2.0 (Germany) and P028 / WCAG 2.1 (Switzerland).

4.9 Cloud Infrastructure and Hosting

Recommended: Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) [3, 11] (Apache 2.0; OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph). Scores: licence 5, maturity 4 (multiple Länder), interoperability 5, security 5 (BSI IT-Grundschutz compatible).

Procurement pathways:

  1. Self-hosted SCS (cities >500,000 with in-house ops)
  2. SCS-certified commercial hoster (plusserver, OSISM)
  3. govdigital eG shared infrastructure [23] — cooperative, framework-agreement-compliant
  4. ZenDiS OpenDesk SaaS [47] (application layer; Germany only)
  5. Cantonal IT cooperative (Switzerland)

5. Implementation Roadmap

A 36-month, five-phase programme. Small municipalities should use the parallel track in Section 5.6.

Phase 0: Foundation (Months 1–3)

Objective: Establish governance, assess current state, build the coalition.

  • Digital Transformation Steering Committee established
  • Current-state technology audit (software inventory, licence costs, data map)
  • Procurement framework established; MOU with cooperative IT provider
  • Data protection impact assessment initiated
  • Budget envelope approved (indicative: €200,000–€500,000 for Phase 0–1)

Phase 1: Identity and Infrastructure (Months 4–12)

Objective: Stand up the foundational layers.

  • SCS environment operational (own / SCS-certified hoster / govdigital eG / ZenDiS)
  • Keycloak deployed and federated with BundID / Swiss e-ID
  • Nextcloud baseline or OpenDesk SaaS deployed for staff
  • Matrix/Element messaging operational
  • BSI IT-Grundschutz / Swiss ISDS baseline documentation complete
  • SBOM initiated for all open-source components

Phase 2: Services and Workflows (Months 10–18)

Objective: Migrate or build first five citizen-facing services.

  • Five highest-volume services on Camunda/XÖV (DE) or Camunda/eCH (CH)
  • TYPO3 or Drupal CMS migration complete
  • CKAN open data portal launched with first 20 DCAT-AP datasets
  • Decidim or CONSUL Democracy for first participatory process
  • GIS layer operational (QGIS + GeoServer + swisstopo/BKG base data)

Phase 3: Integration and Extension (Months 16–24)

Objective: Integrate all layers; 80% service coverage.

  • All services federated via Keycloak SSO
  • 80% of administrative services digitised; inter-agency XÖV/eCH data exchange
  • Smart city IoT data management aligned with EU Data Act [51]
  • Data Governance Act registration if appropriate [52]

Phase 4: Optimisation and Community (Months 22–30)

Objective: Optimise UX; contribute upstream; publish results.

  • User satisfaction survey (NPS target >40)
  • First contribution to openCode.de / upstream projects
  • Community of practice with ≥3 peer municipalities
  • TCO report and accessibility audit (WCAG 2.1 AA / BITV 2.0) published

Phase 5: Sovereignty and Scale (Months 28–36)

Objective: Full digital sovereignty; replication playbook.

  • Full SBOM audit; sovereign data residency verified (100% sovereign infrastructure)
  • Replication playbook published for neighbouring municipalities
  • Strategy paper v1.0 released publicly under CC-BY-4.0
  • Contribution to GovStack building-block community [48]

5.6 Small Municipality Implementation Track

Guiding principle: Participate in shared platforms; don't build alone.

  • Track 0 (Months 1–2): Join govdigital eG (Germany) or cantonal IT cooperative (Switzerland). Single step providing access to SCS-based infrastructure and framework contracts.
  • Track 1 (Months 2–6): Adopt OpenDesk SaaS from ZenDiS [47] (Germany) or cantonal equivalent. Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element + Collabora in one managed service.
  • Track 2 (Months 4–9): Adopt EfA services from openCode.de [10] (Germany) or eCH-compliant cantonal services (Switzerland) before building bespoke solutions.
  • Track 3 (Months 6–18): Deploy Decidim or CONSUL Democracy; launch CKAN or contribute to cantonal/national portal; integrate Keycloak with BundID or Swiss e-ID.

Key difference: Defer self-hosted SCS and bespoke workflow development to Track 5 (Year 3+), if at all. Cooperative infrastructure achieves sovereignty without self-hosting overhead.

Procurement principles

  1. Procure services, not licences. Pay for implementation, hosting, support, training.
  2. Use cooperative procurement structures. govdigital eG [23] and cantonal IT cooperatives provide GWB/BöB-compliant framework agreements. ZenDiS provides direct SaaS pathway [47].
  3. "Public Money? Public Code!" [4]. All custom software must be released under OSI-approved licence and published on openCode.de. Mandatory contract condition.
  4. Evaluate 5-year TCO. Reference Gendarmerie (40% reduction) [53] and Toulouse (€1.8M savings) [54] in procurement justification.
  5. Require interoperability standards. XÖV [46] (DE), eCH [49] (CH), DCAT-AP [22], GovStack [48]. Non-compliance: disqualifying, not merely a preference.
  6. EU Data Act compliance. Smart city infrastructure procurement must include explicit Data Act [51] compliance requirements.

Change management

Fraunhofer IESE [58] and Bolzano-Bozen experience confirm: personnel resistance, not technical failure, is the primary risk. Appoint a Digital Transformation Champion at senior political level; establish departmental open-source champions; run parallel operations ≥3 months; ensure training budget ≥15% of total project budget; publish public transparency dashboard.

The Munich Cautionary Case

Munich's LiMux project (2003–2017) was reversed primarily due to: political leadership change with closer vendor ties; inadequate change management; compatibility issues with Länder systems. Technical implementation was broadly successful [30]. CONSUL Democracy Foundation (2019) offers a positive counterpoint: community governance survived political change in Madrid. Municipalities should build governance structures that outlast political cycles.

Security Considerations

BSI IT-Grundschutz [26]: All components must receive security updates with documented patch management. AAL2 for citizen-facing services (FIDO2/passkeys via Keycloak). TLS 1.3 minimum; data at rest encrypted for sensitive categories. SBOM maintained quarterly. Incident response plan aligned with NIS2 [27]. Open-source stacks provide better NIS2 security posture than closed-source alternatives — vulnerabilities are publicly disclosed and community-patched.

8. Conclusion

The evidence reviewed in this paper converges on five findings:

1. Open-source municipal technology stacks are technically mature and production-proven. Every functional requirement of a modern municipal government can be met by open-source software with documented deployments at peer municipalities.

2. The regulatory environment increasingly mandates open-source and interoperability. EMBAG (Switzerland), OZG 2.0 (Germany), the Interoperable Europe Act (EU), the EU Data Act (applies September 2025), and NIS2 create a dense regulatory fabric that makes proprietary, vendor-locked systems progressively harder to keep compliant.

3. The economic case is compelling when total costs are counted correctly. The French Gendarmerie's 40% TCO reduction across 103,164 workstations, the City of Toulouse's €1.8 million in three-year savings, and the consistent pattern of 30–40% cost reductions across European government open-source migrations provide robust benchmarks.

4. Success requires as much political and organisational investment as technical investment. Personnel resistance to change, not technical failure, is the primary risk. Clear political mandate, skilled change management, and sustained stakeholder engagement are the binding constraints.

5. The institutional ecosystem is now mature enough to support municipal implementation at scale. ZenDiS's OpenDesk (launched end of 2024), govdigital eG's SCS-based cooperative cloud, openCode.de's 300+ repositories, and the GovStack building-block framework together provide German municipalities with a complete, sovereign digital infrastructure pathway. Swiss municipalities have analogous support from the e-ID Act (summer 2026 rollout), eCH standards, opendata.swiss, and cantonal IT cooperatives.

Cities that move first will benefit from first-mover advantages: shaping cooperative standards, building in-house expertise, contributing to the open-source commons, and acquiring compliance capital that later movers will pay more to obtain. The invitation is open.
