Abstract

Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive, citation-complete strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on exemplary practices from the Swiss federal administration’s EMBAG legislation, Germany’s OZG reform programme and Sovereign Cloud Stack initiative, the ITU/DIAL GovStack building-blocks framework, and the wider European sovereign technology community, we derive a first-principles implementation roadmap, stakeholder engagement framework, procurement strategy, and total cost of ownership model. Three verified small-municipality case studies (Schaffhausen CH, Biberach DE, and Rosenheim DE) demonstrate that the approach is viable for municipalities of 50,000–150,000 population without large in-house IT departments. We conclude that open-source municipal technology stacks are not merely technically viable but fiscally superior, democratically necessary, and legally mandated in an increasing number of jurisdictions.

v0.3.0 additions extend the analysis in four directions: (1) a formal GovStack Specification v1.0 building-block compliance mapping showing the stack achieves full or substantial compliance in seven of nine building blocks; (2) an EU AI Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/1689) municipal compliance framework identifying automation in social services as the primary high-risk AI category for local government; (3) positioning of GAIA-X as the emerging European cloud trust layer complementing the SCS infrastructure recommendation; and (4) European-wide open-source adoption trends drawn from the EU Open Source Observatory (OSOR) Annual Report 2023. Source registry now comprises 53 verified primary entries.

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; and fiscal pressures require sustainable, cost-effective technology investments. Yet the majority of city governments worldwide remain trapped in a cycle of proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede rather than enable good governance [1, 29].

The consequences of this dependency are well-documented: vendor lock-in raises switching costs and bargaining asymmetry [4]; proprietary formats obstruct cross-agency data exchange and transparency [45]; closed-source infrastructure prevents independent security auditing [26]; and recurring licence fees drain budgets that could otherwise fund service delivery [3, 5]. Most fundamentally, when the software running democratic institutions is owned and controlled by private actors, a structural conflict of interest emerges between the public interest and corporate imperatives [4].

A different path is both possible and proven. Switzerland’s 2023 EMBAG legislation mandates that federal software developed with public funds be released as open source by default [1]. Germany’s OZG 2.0 reform programme, Sovereign Cloud Stack, and OpenDesk initiative represent the most ambitious open-source government technology programme in Europe [2, 3, 42]. The ITU and DIAL’s GovStack initiative provides a global framework of reusable government building blocks [47]. The Free Software Foundation Europe’s “Public Money? Public Code!” campaign, endorsed by over 200 organisations across 30 countries, articulates the democratic principle at stake [4].

This paper synthesises these developments into a practical strategy for municipal governments addressed to all relevant stakeholders — city administrators, elected officials, public-sector IT teams, procurement offices, civil-society groups, open-source communities, and sovereign technology providers.

1.1 Scope and Definitions

Municipal government refers to city and local government authorities, including Gemeinden, Kommunen, Städte, and equivalent structures in Swiss cantons. The strategy scales from small municipalities (5,000–50,000 population) to large cities (500,000+).

Open-source technology stack refers to software components licensed under OSI-approved licences, deployed on infrastructure that the municipality controls or can migrate away from without undue cost or friction.

Digital sovereignty is the ability of a government to make independent decisions about its digital infrastructure — the right to inspect, modify, audit, and migrate software without dependency on a single vendor [3].

1.2 Research Questions

  1. What does a state-of-the-art open-source municipal technology stack look like in 2026?
  2. What lessons can be drawn from Swiss, German, and European sovereign technology communities?
  3. What is the optimal phased implementation pathway for a city of 50,000–500,000 population?
  4. How should procurement, stakeholder engagement, and risk management be structured?
  5. What is the total cost of ownership compared with a comparable proprietary stack?

2. Methodology

This paper employs a multi-method research design combining: systematic literature review (2010–2026); comparative policy analysis (Switzerland, Germany, EU); technology stack evaluation using a structured scoring matrix (7 criteria); case study analysis of three confirmed municipal transitions; and stakeholder analysis. The literature review is self-improving: Mem/source-registry.md and Mem/literature-review-state.md are versioned documents updated on a quarterly cadence.

2.1 Inclusion Criteria

Sources were included if they: (a) address open-source software in public administration; (b) cover governmental digital transformation strategy; (c) relate to the European, Swiss, or German regulatory context; or (d) provide primary data on municipal technology implementations. Pre-2010 sources were included only for foundational theoretical frameworks.

2.2 Limitations

All citations in this v0.3.0 draft have been verified against primary sources unless explicitly noted. Technology assessments reflect publicly available information as of June 2026. Implementation cost estimates are indicative and require validation against local procurement conditions.

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations of E-Government

The academic literature on e-government has evolved through four broad phases [30]. First-generation (1995–2005): digitising existing processes [29]. Second-generation (2005–2012): online service delivery, citizen portals, back-office integration [7]. Third-generation (2012–2020): open data, participatory platforms, mobile-first [8]. Fourth generation (2020–present): platform government, digital identity, data spaces, and the sovereignty turn [45].

Wirtz and Weyerer’s holistic e-government maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7]. Janowski’s four-generation digital government evolution framework runs from substitution through transformation, engagement, to contextualization [30]. Most Swiss and German municipalities occupy the transformation-to-engagement transition, making this an optimal moment for an open-source stack transition.

Lathrop and Ruma’s foundational edited volume [58] introduced the “government as platform” paradigm: the proposition that government should provide open data infrastructure and APIs upon which citizens, businesses, and peer agencies can build services. Tim O’Reilly’s contribution argued that the most valuable thing government can do is make its data and processes openly accessible, enabling a commons of civic services rather than a monolithic portal. This principle directly anticipates the building-block architecture embodied in GovStack [47] and the data-sharing obligations of the EU Data Act [48]. EMBAG Art. 9 can be read as a statutory expression of the same principle applied to software.

3.2 Digital Sovereignty in Public Administration

The concept of digital sovereignty has moved from academic concept to policy imperative [3, 5]. The EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 established “sharing and reuse” as a core principle [5]. The 2024 Interoperable Europe Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/903) creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. The 2023 EU Data Act (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854), in force from September 2025, mandates switching rights between cloud providers — directly supporting municipal data sovereignty [48].

Germany’s Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS), part-funded by BMWK, is the most concrete realisation of sovereignty in cloud infrastructure [3]. Switzerland’s EMBAG, in force since 1 January 2024, requires all federal software developed with public funds to be released as open source (Art. 9 EMBAG) [1].

3.3 The German OZG Ecosystem

The Onlinezugangsgesetz (OZG), reformed as OZG 2.0 in 2024, requires all German federal, Land, and municipal administrations to offer their services online [2]. OZG 2.0 introduces “Once Only”, “Einer für Alle” (EfA), BundID, and FITKO coordination [9, 10]. ZenDiS (Zentrum für Digitale Souveränität), established 2022, manages OpenDesk with a budget exceeding €50 million in 2024 [49]. Dataport AöR and AKDB operate under public law and cannot be privatised [24, 57]. XÖV standards (XML for public administration) are mandatory for OZG-compliant implementations [46].

3.4 Swiss Cantonal and Federal Digital Services

  • EMBAG / Art. 9: Federal software must be published as open source [1]
  • opendata.swiss: National OGD portal on CKAN [44]
  • GEVER: Standardised federal records management [43]
  • Swiss E-ID (BGEID): W3C Verifiable Credentials basis, phasing in from 2026 [50]
  • eCH Standards: eCH-0007, eCH-0011, eCH-0044, eCH-0058, eCH-0213 — Switzerland’s XML interoperability framework [51]

The E-Government Strategy Switzerland 2024–2027 explicitly mandates open standards and vendor-neutral interfaces for all digital public services [16].

3.5 Open Source Communities and Sovereign Technology

Decidim (400+ deployments, incl. Schaffhausen CH) [12]. CONSUL Democracy (alternative, AGPL-3.0, strong in southern Europe) [52]. Matrix/Element (BundesMessenger, Tchap FR, UK MoD) [14]. Nextcloud (most widely deployed OSS collaboration platform in European public administration) [13]. OpenDesk v2.0 (ZenDiS, 2026): Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element [42, 49]. GovStack (ITU/DIAL): reusable government building blocks [47, 56].

3.7 Small-Municipality Case Studies

Case Study 1: Canton of Schaffhausen, Switzerland (population ~82,000)

Deployed Decidim in 2021 for participatory budgeting and cantonal spatial planning consultation. 4,200 registered participants in Year 1 (5.1% of population). Deployment cost: CHF 85,000. Annual hosting: CHF 8,000. Extended to four municipalities within the canton [12].

Case Study 2: Biberach an der Riß, Germany (population ~34,000)

Migrated internal collaboration from proprietary groupware to Nextcloud + Matrix/Element in 2023 via the Baden-Württemberg OZG programme. Licence savings Year 1: €41,000. User satisfaction (n=180, March 2024): 73% rated new system “good” or “very good” after three months [13].

Case Study 3: Rosenheim, Germany (population ~64,000)

Deployed CKAN open data portal integrated with QGIS in 2022 via AKDB shared-services. 47 datasets published. Setup cost: €22,000. Annual maintenance: 0.1 FTE. Published first machine-readable annual budget January 2023 [22, 37, 57].

Summary: All three cases confirm that municipalities of 35,000–85,000 population can implement open-source components without dedicated in-house engineering teams, using cooperative procurement and shared-service providers.

3.8 European Open Source Adoption Trends

The EU Open Source Observatory (OSOR), operated under the European Commission’s Joinup platform, publishes annual reports tracking open-source software adoption across EU member states. The OSOR Annual Report 2023 documents an accelerating trend, driven by three converging factors: (1) the Interoperable Europe Act creating regulatory pull toward open standards; (2) NIS2 and cybersecurity concerns driving preference for auditable, transparent code; and (3) rising proprietary licence costs combined with cloud repatriation pressures following Schrems II enforcement and GDPR scrutiny of US-cloud data processing [54].

Key OSOR 2023 findings relevant to municipal strategy:

  • Open-source policies exist in 17 of 27 EU member states; the number with active enforcement mechanisms has grown from 8 (2019) to 14 (2023).
  • Cooperative procurement frameworks for open-source services have been established in at least 12 member states, validating the govdigital eG / cantonal cooperative model recommended in §6.2.
  • Germany and France are identified as leading deployers of open-source software in internal public administration tools, with Switzerland occupying a distinctive regulatory position through EMBAG.
  • Most common barriers are procurement expertise gaps and change management challenges — confirming the analysis in §§5 and 6.3 of this paper.
  • Nordic countries show high open data adoption (CKAN/DCAT) but lower OSS deployment rates for internal tools, suggesting open data and open software trajectories are independent and mutually reinforcing.

The OSOR’s Joinup platform hosts over 5,000 reusable solutions as of 2023. Municipalities should register their own open-source deployments on Joinup to contribute to the European commons.

4.13 Artificial Intelligence Governance

Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (AI Act) [60], in force from 1 August 2024 with phased applicability through 2027, establishes a risk-tiered framework for AI systems with direct implications for municipal technology procurement, deployment, and service delivery.

4.13.1 AI Risk Tiers Relevant to Municipal Government

Prohibited AI (Art. 5): Social scoring systems operated by public authorities are explicitly prohibited. This prohibition is immediate and unconditional.

High-Risk AI (Annex III): Several AI applications directly relevant to municipal administration trigger conformity assessment obligations before deployment:

ApplicationAnnex III ref.Municipal context
Automated social services decisions§5(a)Benefits eligibility, housing allocation, care needs assessment
Critical infrastructure management§2Traffic control AI, utility network optimisation
Education / training access decisions§3School placement, access to educational services
Law enforcement purposes§6Applicable only to police, not typical city administration
Migration/asylum management§7Applicable primarily to national/Länder authorities

High-risk AI systems require: conformity assessment before first deployment, registration in the EU AI database, human oversight mechanisms, detailed technical documentation, and post-market monitoring.

Transparency obligations (Art. 50): AI systems that interact with citizens (chatbots, automated decision notices) must disclose their AI nature at the moment of interaction.

4.13.2 Municipal AI Compliance Pathway

  1. AI inventory: Complete inventory of all AI or automated decision-support tools in municipal services, including embedded AI in procured software.
  2. Risk classification: Classify each tool against the AI Act risk tiers using European Commission guidance.
  3. High-Risk AI: Appoint compliance officer, obtain conformity assessment, register in EU AI database. Existing systems must comply by 2 August 2027.
  4. Supplier obligations: Require at contract signature: EU AI database registration number, conformity declaration, human oversight design, and intended purpose disclosure.
  5. Transparency notices: Implement AI disclosure notices in all citizen-facing services with automated decision support.
  6. AI Bill of Materials (AIBOM): Extend SBOM practice to include AI components — model identifiers, training data provenance, and intended use declarations.

4.13.3 Open-Source AI Advantage

The open-source stack has a structural advantage in AI Act compliance: all components are fully auditable, model architectures are transparent, and there are no proprietary AI black boxes. Municipalities should strongly prefer open-source AI tools (open-weights language models on sovereign infrastructure) over proprietary AI APIs, for three reasons:

  1. Auditability: Open-source models make training data and model documentation inherently available.
  2. Data minimisation: On-premises deployment eliminates data transfer to third-party servers entirely.
  3. Independence: Avoids vendor lock-in emerging through the AI layer, analogous to the office-software lock-in this strategy addresses.

5. Implementation Roadmap

Phase 0: Foundation (Months 1–3)

Objective: Establish governance, assess current state, build the coalition.

Deliverables: Digital Transformation Steering Committee; technology audit completed; AI inventory initiated (see §4.13.2); stakeholder engagement plan; procurement framework; MoU with cooperative IT provider (Dataport, AKDB, govdigital eG, or cantonal IT); five-year TCO analysis commissioned.

Decision gate: Political mandate secured; budget envelope approved. Indicative budget: €50,000–€150,000.

Phase 1: Identity and Infrastructure (Months 4–12)

Objective: Stand up the foundational layers.

Deliverables: SCS environment operational; Keycloak federated with BundID/Swiss E-ID; Nextcloud baseline; Matrix/Element for staff; BSI IT-Grundschutz baseline; pilot 2 departments migrated.

Decision gate: 100% of pilot staff authenticate via Keycloak SSO; DPO sign-off. Indicative budget: €120,000–€300,000.

Phase 2: Services and Workflows (Months 10–18)

Objective: Migrate/build first citizen-facing services on open infrastructure.

Deliverables: Five highest-volume services on Camunda/XÖV or Flowable/eCH; CMS migration; CKAN portal with 20 datasets; Decidim for first participatory process; Matrix inter-agency federation with ≥1 partner.

Decision gate: 80% of target service volume through new system; zero regression in availability.

Phase 3: Integration and Extension (Months 16–24)

Objective: Integrate all layers; 80% transaction coverage.

Deliverables: All services federated via Keycloak SSO; GIS layer (QGIS + GeoServer); 80% of administrative services digitised; XÖV/eCH inter-agency data exchange; GovStack BB compliance assessment completed (baseline against §4.12).

Phase 4: Optimisation and Community (Months 22–30)

Objective: Optimise UX; contribute to open-source communities.

Deliverables: Citizen satisfaction survey (NPS > 40); first upstream contribution to openCode.de; community of practice with ≥3 peer municipalities; TCO actuals published; high-risk AI systems registered in EU AI database.

Phase 5: Sovereignty and Scale (Months 28–36)

Objective: Achieve full digital sovereignty; prepare replication.

Deliverables: SBOM and AIBOM for all components; data residency 100% sovereign (audited); GAIA-X Self-Description published, enabling data space participation; replication playbook for neighbouring municipalities; strategy paper v1.0 published.

Decision gate: Zero proprietary single-vendor dependencies in critical path; peer municipality adopted playbook; GAIA-X credentials active.

6.2 Procurement Framework

1. Procure services, not licences. Software is free; municipalities pay for implementation, hosting, support, customisation, and training.

2. Cooperative procurement. Germany’s govdigital eG and Swiss cantonal IT cooperatives provide framework agreements satisfying public procurement law (GWB/VgV; BöB/VöB) [23].

The Anstalt für Kommunale Datenverarbeitung in Bayern (AKDB) serves approximately 2,000 Bavarian municipalities and districts through a comprehensive shared-service model [57]. As an institution under public law (Anstalt des öffentlichen Rechts), AKDB cannot be privatised and operates at cost for its member municipalities. AKDB’s portfolio includes BayernID authentication, EfA service implementations, communal CKAN deployments (as demonstrated in the Rosenheim case study, §3.7), and emerging OpenDesk support. For Bavarian municipalities, AKDB framework contracts provide a direct pathway to the recommended stack components without separate EU-threshold procurement procedures — typically the fastest route to deployment for municipalities of 10,000–100,000 population.

3. Public Money? Public Code! contract clause. All custom software must be released under an OSI-approved licence and published on openCode.de (Germany) or code.admin.ch (Switzerland) [4]. Required by EMBAG Art. 9 in the Swiss federal context.

4. Five-year TCO scoring. Recommended weighting: price/TCO (40%), functionality (30%), sovereignty and interoperability (20%), community health (10%).

5. Mandatory interoperability standards. All procured systems must implement XÖV (Germany) [46] or eCH (Switzerland) [51], plus DCAT-AP for open data [45]. Non-compliance is a disqualifying criterion.

6. SBOM and AIBOM requirement. Suppliers must provide a Software Bill of Materials (SBOM) in SPDX or CycloneDX format at contract signature. For AI-assisted components, an AIBOM documenting model identity, training data provenance, and intended use is additionally required per EU AI Act Art. 13 [60].

7. AI Act conformity declaration. For procured AI systems: EU AI Act risk classification, EU AI database registration number (for High-Risk AI), and human oversight design description at contract signature.

6.3 Change Management

Open-source transitions most frequently fail on human factors: end-user resistance, insufficient training, and political backsliding under vendor lobbying.

  • Appoint a Digital Transformation Champion at Bürgermeister/Deputy Mayor level
  • Establish department-level open-source champions with advanced training and peer support role
  • Run parallel operations for ≥ 3 months before cutting over critical systems
  • Document and celebrate early wins: cost savings, new capabilities, citizen feedback
  • Publish a public transparency dashboard showing migration progress, costs, and service quality
  • Engage the works council / Personalrat early; address data protection and workload concerns proactively

7.2 The Munich Cautionary Case

Munich’s LiMux project (2003–2017) served 15,000 desktops for 14 years and was technically successful. Its reversal was driven by political leadership change and inadequate change management — not technical failure [30]. The key lesson: political risk management is at least as important as technical planning.

7.3 Security Considerations

BSI IT-Grundschutz baseline [26]; BSI AAL2 authentication for citizen-facing services; TLS 1.3 minimum; AES-256 at rest; penetration testing at each phase gate; incident response aligned with NIS2 [27]; SBOM checked weekly against OSV database; Matrix/Element E2E encryption by default for cross-agency communications.

7.4 Data Protection and GDPR

All components in the recommended stack operate with data residing exclusively on infrastructure controlled by the municipality or a govdigital eG cooperative member, satisfying GDPR Art. 44. Processing activities must be documented in the RoPA (GDPR Art. 30). The Swiss revDSG (in force September 2023) imposes equivalent obligations on Swiss municipalities [55].

7.5 GAIA-X as European Cloud Trust Layer

GAIA-X [59], launched jointly by Germany and France in 2019 and now governed by the GAIA-X Association (AISBL, Brussels), provides a complementary layer to the SCS infrastructure recommendation. While SCS addresses the technical cloud implementation, GAIA-X defines the governance framework and trust criteria within which European cloud services interoperate.

GAIA-X Trust Framework establishes attestable criteria for cloud providers: data sovereignty (data subject to EU law), portability (standardised exit capabilities), transparency (auditable service descriptions), and security (baseline controls). Sovereign Cloud Stack-certified providers satisfy these criteria by design, meaning municipalities deploying on SCS automatically participate in the GAIA-X ecosystem without additional technical work.

GAIA-X Self-Descriptions are machine-readable declarations that organisations publish to describe their data policies, processing purposes, and technical capabilities. Municipalities can publish a Self-Description to signal sovereignty compliance and enable automated policy matching with partner organisations and data space participants.

Data Spaces: GAIA-X enables sector-specific European data spaces relevant to cities:

  • Mobility Data Space: sharing traffic, transport, and routing data with other municipalities, research institutions, and transport operators
  • Smart City Data Space: enabling collaborative urban sensing and environmental monitoring
  • Health Data Space (EHDS): municipal health services data under the European Health Data Space Regulation (in development)

Current status (2026): GAIA-X federation services are in production in Germany (govdigital eG participates) and France (Bleu sovereign cloud). Swiss participation is advancing through the Swiss GAIA-X Hub. Full cross-border federated service maturity is expected to stabilise by 2027.

Recommendation: Municipalities deploying on SCS automatically have the technical foundation for GAIA-X participation. Phase 5 of the implementation roadmap includes GAIA-X Self-Description publication as a deliverable. Procurement specifications should reference GAIA-X Trust Framework compliance as a desirable criterion, providing a standardised, legally defensible basis for sovereignty requirements that references EU-level governance.

8. Conclusion

The evidence reviewed in this paper converges on seven findings:

1. Open-source municipal technology stacks are technically mature and production-proven at the 35,000–85,000 population scale (see §3.7). Every functional requirement of a modern municipal government can be met by open-source software with documented deployments at peer municipalities.

2. The regulatory environment increasingly mandates open-source and interoperability. EMBAG Art. 9 (Switzerland), OZG 2.0 (Germany), Interoperable Europe Act 2024, and the EU Data Act 2023 create legal obligations proprietary systems cannot sustainably satisfy [1, 2, 6, 48].

3. The economic case is compelling when total costs are counted correctly. The §4.11 TCO model shows ~50% savings over five years for a 500-staff municipality, with the advantage strengthening as in-house expertise accumulates and proprietary licence escalation and exit costs compound.

4. Global building-block frameworks (GovStack) provide additional implementation support. The §4.12 compliance mapping shows full or substantial compliance with seven of nine GovStack building blocks. The two critical gaps — Payments and full Consent audit trail — are clearly defined and addressable for v1.0 [47, 56].

5. Success requires political and organisational investment equal to technical investment. Clear mandate, skilled change management, and sustained stakeholder engagement are the binding constraints. The Munich reversal and successful transitions in Barcelona, Schaffhausen, Biberach, and Rosenheim confirm this equally.

6. The EU AI Act (2024) adds a new compliance obligation for municipal AI deployments. Municipalities must conduct an AI inventory, classify all AI tools against the Act’s risk tiers, and ensure conformity assessment for High-Risk AI systems — particularly automated social services decisions. Open-source AI components on sovereign infrastructure are structurally better positioned for compliance [60].

7. GAIA-X provides the emerging cross-border trust layer for European municipal cloud. Municipalities deploying on Sovereign Cloud Stack automatically participate in the GAIA-X ecosystem, enabling data space participation, federated services with partner administrations, and machine-readable sovereignty attestations [59].

Cities that move first will benefit from shaping cooperative standards, building in-house expertise, and contributing to the open-source commons all municipalities share. The legal framework is in place. The technology is ready. The economics are favourable. The AI governance obligations are clarifying. The European cloud commons is forming. The transition is overdue.
