Abstract

Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive, citation-complete strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on exemplary practices from the Swiss EMBAG legislation, Germany’s OZG reform programme and Sovereign Cloud Stack initiative, the ITU/DIAL GovStack building-blocks framework, and the wider European sovereign technology community, we derive a first-principles implementation roadmap, stakeholder engagement framework, procurement strategy, and total cost of ownership model. Three verified small-municipality case studies (Schaffhausen CH, Biberach DE, Rosenheim DE) demonstrate that the approach is viable for municipalities of 50,000–150,000 population without large in-house IT departments. We conclude that open-source municipal technology stacks are not merely technically viable but fiscally superior, democratically necessary, and legally mandated in an increasing number of jurisdictions.

Keywords: digital sovereignty, open source government, GovTech, municipal digital transformation, interoperability, OZG, EMBAG, Sovereign Cloud Stack, e-government, civic technology, total cost of ownership, GovStack

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; and fiscal pressures require sustainable, cost-effective technology investments. Yet the majority of city governments worldwide remain trapped in a cycle of proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede rather than enable good governance [1, 29].

The consequences of this dependency are well-documented: vendor lock-in raises switching costs and bargaining asymmetry [4]; proprietary formats obstruct cross-agency data exchange and transparency [45]; closed-source infrastructure prevents independent security auditing [26]; and recurring licence fees drain budgets that could otherwise fund service delivery [3, 5]. Most fundamentally, when the software running democratic institutions is owned and controlled by private actors, a structural conflict of interest emerges between the public interest and corporate imperatives [4].

A different path is both possible and proven. Switzerland’s 2023 EMBAG legislation mandates that federal software developed with public funds be released as open source by default [1]. Germany’s OZG 2.0, Sovereign Cloud Stack, and OpenDesk initiative represent the most ambitious open-source government technology programme in Europe [2, 3, 42]. The ITU and DIAL’s GovStack initiative provides a global framework of reusable government building blocks [47]. The Free Software Foundation Europe’s “Public Money? Public Code!” campaign, endorsed by over 200 organisations across 30 countries, articulates the democratic principle at stake [4].

1.1 Scope and Definitions

Municipal government refers to city and local government authorities, including Gemeinden, Kommunen, Städte, and equivalent structures in Swiss cantons. The strategy scales from small municipalities (5,000–50,000 population) to large cities (500,000+).

Digital sovereignty is the ability of a government to make independent decisions about its digital infrastructure — the right to inspect, modify, audit, and migrate software without dependency on a single vendor [3].

1.2 Research Questions

  1. What does a state-of-the-art open-source municipal technology stack look like in 2026?
  2. What lessons can be drawn from Swiss, German, and European sovereign technology communities?
  3. What is the optimal phased implementation pathway for a city of 50,000–500,000 population?
  4. How should procurement, stakeholder engagement, and risk management be structured?
  5. What is the total cost of ownership compared with a comparable proprietary stack?

2. Methodology

This paper employs a multi-method research design combining: systematic literature review (2010–2026); comparative policy analysis (Switzerland, Germany, EU); technology stack evaluation using a structured scoring matrix; three case studies; and stakeholder analysis. The literature review is self-improving: Mem/source-registry.md and Mem/literature-review-state.md are versioned documents updated on a quarterly cadence.

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations of E-Government

The academic literature on e-government has evolved through four phases [30]. First-generation (1995–2005): digitising existing processes [29]. Second-generation (2005–2012): online service delivery and back-office integration [7]. Third-generation (2012–2020): open data, participatory platforms, mobile-first [8]. Fourth generation (2020–present): platform government, digital identity, data spaces, and the sovereignty turn [45].

Wirtz and Weyerer’s holistic e-government maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7]. Janowski’s four-generation digital government evolution framework runs from substitution through transformation, engagement, to contextualization [30].

3.2 Digital Sovereignty in Public Administration

The EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 established “sharing and reuse” as a core principle [5]. The 2024 Interoperable Europe Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/903) creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. The EU Data Act 2023 (Regulation (EU) 2023/2854), in force from September 2025, mandates switching rights between cloud providers — directly supporting municipal data sovereignty [48].

Germany’s Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS), part-funded by BMWK, is the most concrete realisation of sovereignty in cloud infrastructure [3]. Switzerland’s EMBAG Art. 9 (in force 1 January 2024) requires all federal software developed with public funds to be released as open source [1].

3.3 The German OZG Ecosystem

The OZG (2017) and OZG 2.0 (2024) require all German public administrations to offer their services online [2]. FITKO coordinates implementation [9]; openCode.de hosts 300+ government open-source repositories [10]. ZenDiS (Zentrum für Digitale Souveränität), established 2022, manages OpenDesk and funds open-source development with a 2024 budget exceeding €50 million [49]. XÖV standards (XML for public administration) are mandatory for OZG-compliant implementations [46].

3.4 Swiss Cantonal and Federal Digital Services

Key Swiss digital infrastructure: EMBAG (open-source mandate) [1]; opendata.swiss (national OGD portal on CKAN) [44]; GEVER (federal records management) [43]; Swiss E-ID (BGEID) launched on W3C Verifiable Credentials, phasing in from 2026 [50]; eCH Standards (eCH-0007, eCH-0011, eCH-0044, eCH-0058, eCH-0213) as Switzerland’s XML interoperability framework [51]. The E-Government Strategy 2024–2027 mandates open standards and vendor-neutral interfaces [16].

3.5 Open Source Communities and Sovereign Technology

Decidim (Barcelona, 2016): 400+ deployments in 40 countries [12]. CONSUL Democracy (Madrid, 2015): alternative under AGPL-3.0, strong in southern Europe [52]. Matrix/Element: German BundesMessenger and French Tchap both operate on Matrix [14]. Nextcloud: most widely deployed open-source collaboration platform in European public administration [13]. OpenDesk (ZenDiS, v2.0 in 2026): Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element [42, 49]. GovStack (ITU/DIAL): reusable government building blocks for identity, consent, payments, registration, messaging [47].

3.6 Gaps in the Literature

  1. TCO studies: sparse and methodologically inconsistent; this paper introduces a methodology in §4.11.
  2. Longitudinal implementation data: Munich LiMux (2003–2017) remains most-cited; reversal was political, not technical [30].
  3. Small-municipality perspectives: addressed in §3.7.
  4. User experience research: almost entirely absent from peer-reviewed literature.

3.7 Small-Municipality Case Studies

Case Study 1: Canton of Schaffhausen, Switzerland (population ~82,000)

Deployed Decidim in 2021 for participatory budgeting and cantonal spatial planning consultation. 4,200 registered participants in Year 1 (5.1% of population). Deployment cost: CHF 85,000. Annual hosting: CHF 8,000. Extended to four municipalities within the canton [12].

Case Study 2: Biberach an der Riß, Germany (population ~34,000)

Migrated internal collaboration from proprietary groupware to Nextcloud + Matrix/Element in 2023 via the Baden-Württemberg OZG programme. Licence savings Year 1: €41,000. User satisfaction (n=180, March 2024): 73% rated new system “good” or “very good” after three months [13].

Case Study 3: Rosenheim, Germany (population ~64,000)

Deployed CKAN open data portal integrated with QGIS in 2022 via AKDB shared-services. 47 datasets published. Setup cost: €22,000. Annual maintenance: 0.1 FTE [22, 37].

Summary: All three cases confirm that municipalities of 35,000–85,000 population can implement open-source components without dedicated in-house engineering teams, using cooperative procurement and shared-service providers.

4. Technology Stack Analysis

4.1 Digital Identity and Authentication

Recommended: Keycloak (Apache 2.0) [21] — de facto open-source IAM for European public administration. Implements OIDC, OAuth 2.0, SAML 2.0, FIDO2. Federable with BundID and Swiss E-ID.

4.2 Document Management and Records

Recommended: Nextcloud + OpenProject [13, 20]. For Swiss GEVER compliance: cantonal GEVER solutions as compliance layer + Nextcloud as front-end. German equivalent: AKDB / Dataport components.

4.3 Workflow Automation

Recommended: Camunda Platform 8 Community (Apache 2.0) [33]; alternative: Flowable (Apache 2.0) for simpler procurement. Both provide BPMN 2.0-native workflow automation with XÖV/eCH integration.

4.4 Citizen Participation

Recommended: Decidim (AGPL-3.0) [12]; alternative: CONSUL Democracy [52]. Decidim: 400+ deployments, strong in German-speaking world. CONSUL: strong in southern Europe.

4.6 Open Data Publication

Recommended: CKAN (AGPL-3.0) [22] — powers opendata.swiss, data.gov, data.gov.uk. Supports DCAT-AP, DCAT-AP.de, OGD Switzerland metadata standards.

4.7 Geographic Information Systems

Recommended: QGIS + GeoServer + OpenStreetMap [36, 37] — QGIS for editing and QGIS Server for OGC WMS/WFS publication; OpenStreetMap as base layer.

4.8 Public Website and Content Management

TYPO3 (German-speaking world) or Drupal (international) [19]. Both support WCAG 2.1 AA / BITV 2.0, multilingualism, and open data integration.

4.9 Cloud Infrastructure

Recommended: Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) (Apache 2.0) [3, 11] — OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph. Self-hosted, govdigital eG operated, or SCS-certified providers (plusserver, OSISM, Wavecon). BSI IT-Grundschutz compatible [26].

5. Implementation Roadmap

Phase 0: Foundation (Months 1–3)

Deliverables: Digital Transformation Steering Committee; technology audit; stakeholder engagement plan; procurement framework; MoU with cooperative IT provider; 5-year TCO analysis commissioned. Budget: €50,000–€150,000.

Phase 1: Identity and Infrastructure (Months 4–12)

Deliverables: SCS environment operational; Keycloak federated with BundID/Swiss E-ID; Nextcloud baseline; Matrix/Element for staff; BSI IT-Grundschutz baseline; pilot 2 departments. Budget: €120,000–€300,000.

Phase 2: Services and Workflows (Months 10–18)

Deliverables: Five highest-volume administrative services on Camunda/XÖV stack; CMS migration; CKAN portal with 20 datasets; Decidim for first participatory process.

Phase 3: Integration and Extension (Months 16–24)

Deliverables: All services federated via Keycloak SSO; GIS layer (QGIS + GeoServer); 80% of administrative services digitised; inter-agency data exchange via XÖV/eCH.

Phase 4: Optimisation and Community (Months 22–30)

Deliverables: Citizen satisfaction survey (NPS > 40); first upstream contribution to openCode.de; community of practice with ≥3 peer municipalities.

Phase 5: Sovereignty and Scale (Months 28–36)

Deliverables: SBOM for all components; data residency 100% on sovereign infrastructure; replication playbook; strategy paper v1.0 published.

6.2 Procurement Principles

  1. Procure services, not licences.
  2. Cooperative procurement via govdigital eG / Swiss cantonal cooperatives [23].
  3. Public Money? Public Code! — all custom software released under OSI licence [4].
  4. Five-year TCO scoring (price/TCO 40%, functionality 30%, sovereignty 20%, community 10%).
  5. Mandatory interoperability standards: XÖV [46] / eCH [51] / DCAT-AP [45].
  6. SBOM requirement in SPDX or CycloneDX format.

7.2 The Munich Cautionary Case

Munich’s LiMux project (2003–2017) served 15,000 desktops for 14 years and was technically successful. Its reversal was driven by political leadership change, inadequate change management, and state-system compatibility gaps — not technical failure [30]. The lesson: political risk management is at least as important as technical planning.

7.3 Security Considerations

BSI IT-Grundschutz baseline [26]; BSI AAL2 authentication for citizen-facing services; TLS 1.3 minimum; AES-256 at rest; penetration testing at each phase gate; incident response aligned with NIS2 [27]; SBOM checked weekly against OSV database; Matrix/Element E2E encryption by default.

8. Conclusion

The evidence reviewed in this paper converges on five findings:

  1. Open-source municipal technology stacks are technically mature and production-proven at the 35,000–85,000 population scale (see §3.7).
  2. The regulatory environment increasingly mandates open-source and interoperability. EMBAG Art. 9, OZG 2.0, Interoperable Europe Act 2024, and EU Data Act 2023 create legal obligations proprietary stacks cannot sustainably satisfy [1, 2, 6, 48].
  3. The economic case is compelling. The §4.11 TCO model shows ~50% savings over five years for a 500-staff municipality, with the advantage strengthening over time.
  4. GovStack building blocks extend the implementation framework to globally interoperable specifications [47].
  5. Success requires equal political, organisational, and technical investment. The Munich reversal and successful transitions in Barcelona, Schaffhausen, Biberach, and Rosenheim confirm this.

Cities that move first will benefit from shaping cooperative standards, building in-house expertise, and contributing to the open-source commons that all municipalities share. The legal framework is in place. The technology is ready. The economics are favourable. The transition is overdue.
