Sovereign by Design: A State-of-the-Art Open-Source Technology Strategy for Municipal Governments

Author: Sebastian Graf, Autonomous Office of Civil Digital Infrastructure

Date: 2026-06-21  ·  Languages: English (source of truth) · Deutsch

Abstract

Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive, citation-complete strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on exemplary practices from the Swiss federal EMBAG legislation [1], Germany’s OZG reform programme [2], Sovereign Cloud Stack [3], and OpenDesk [42], we derive a first-principles implementation roadmap, stakeholder engagement framework, and procurement strategy. A new TCO analysis demonstrates that a 50,000-resident municipality can expect net savings of €400,000–€1,200,000 over five years by transitioning to an open-source stack. Case studies from Barcelona, Helsinki, Canton Schaffhausen, and three small municipalities provide empirical grounding. We conclude that open-source municipal technology stacks are fiscally superior, democratically necessary, and legally mandated in an increasing number of jurisdictions.

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; and fiscal pressures require sustainable, cost-effective technology investments. Yet the majority of city governments worldwide remain trapped in a cycle of proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede rather than enable good governance [29, 60].

The consequences of this dependency are well-documented: vendor lock-in raises switching costs and bargaining asymmetry [4]; proprietary formats obstruct cross-agency data exchange and transparency [45]; closed-source infrastructure prevents independent security auditing [26]; and recurring licence fees drain budgets that could otherwise fund service delivery [3, 5]. Most fundamentally, when the software running democratic institutions is owned by private actors, a structural conflict of interest emerges between the public interest and corporate imperatives [4, 53].

A different path is both possible and proven. Switzerland’s EMBAG legislation (in force 1 January 2024) mandates that federal software developed with public funds be released as open source by default [1]. Germany’s OZG reform, Sovereign Cloud Stack, OpenDesk, and ZenDiS GmbH represent the most ambitious open-source government technology programme in Europe [2, 3, 42, 49]. A 2021 EU Commission study found that open source contributes €65–95 billion annually to EU GDP, and that every €1 invested in open source generates €4.15 in economic return [56].

1.1 Scope and Definitions

Municipal government refers to city and local government authorities, including Gemeinden, Kommunen, Städte (Germany), Gemeinden and Städte (Switzerland), and equivalent structures across German-speaking Europe. The strategy scales from small municipalities (5,000–20,000) to large cities (500,000+), with an explicit small-municipality pathway in Section 5.6.

Digital sovereignty is the ability of a government to make independent decisions about its digital infrastructure — the right to inspect, modify, audit, and migrate software without dependency on a single vendor [3]. In the European context it additionally encompasses GDPR data residency obligations and NIS2 security requirements [27].

Interoperability includes technical, semantic, and organisational interoperability as defined by the European Interoperability Framework (EIF) [45], the XÖV standards (Germany) [46], and the eCH standards (Switzerland) [47].

2. Methodology

This paper employs: (a) systematic literature review of peer-reviewed publications and grey literature 2010–2026; (b) comparative policy analysis of EMBAG [1], OZG 2.0 [2], the Interoperable Europe Act [6], the EU Data Act [51], and the Swiss E-ID Law [54]; (c) structured technology stack scoring; (d) five-year TCO modelling across three municipality-size tiers; (e) six case studies of open-source transitions.

The literature review is self-improving: the source registry (Mem/source-registry.md) and literature review state (Mem/literature-review-state.md) are versioned and updated quarterly by Scripts/update_literature_review.py.

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations

E-government has evolved through four phases [30]: digitisation (Gen 1, 1995–2005), online services (Gen 2, 2005–2012), open data and participation (Gen 3, 2012–2020), and platform government with digital sovereignty (Gen 4, 2020–present). Wirtz and Weyerer’s maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7]. Scholl’s handbook [60] identifies political commitment, administrative capacity, and citizen trust as the three binding constraints on successful transitions. Almeida et al. [59] find that open-source adoption correlates with higher long-term service quality in a 7-year study of 127 EU municipalities.

3.2 Digital Sovereignty in Public Administration

The EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 established “sharing and reuse” as a core procurement principle [5]. The Interoperable Europe Act 2024 creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. The EU Data Act 2023 (in force September 2025) establishes cloud switching rights for public-sector bodies, requiring documented exit plans — a requirement open-source stacks satisfy structurally [51].

Germany’s Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) [3] and ZenDiS GmbH [49] are the two institutional anchors of German open-source digital sovereignty. ZenDiS (established 2022 by BMI) manages OpenDesk, coordinates openCode.de (340+ repositories as of 2026), and has a stated goal of 100% open-source capability for the federal workplace by 2027. Switzerland’s EMBAG [1] and eCH standards [47] provide the legislative and interoperability framework for the Swiss context.

3.3 German OZG Ecosystem

OZG 2.0 (2024) mandates online availability of all administrative services via the “Once Only” and “Einer für Alle” principles, centred on BundID and FITKO [2, 9]. The FITKO 2023 Annual Report documents 439 OZG services in production by December 2023 [9]. openCode.de hosts 340+ government software repositories [10, 57]. XÖV standards (KoSIT) provide mandatory data interchange vocabulary [46].

3.4 Swiss Federal and Cantonal Digital Services

Switzerland’s key digital infrastructure: Fedlex (machine-readable federal law, CC0) [1]; opendata.swiss (national open data portal on CKAN) [44]; GEVER (federal records management) [43]; and the new Swiss E-ID (state-issued, privacy-preserving, SD-JWT-based, expected in production 2026) [54]. The eCH standards framework provides XML interoperability for Swiss municipalities, analogous to Germany’s XÖV [47]. The E-Government Strategy 2024–2027 mandates open standards and interoperability [16].

3.5 Open Source Communities and Sovereign Technology

Decidim (Barcelona, 2016): 400+ deployments globally, including Barcelona, Helsinki, Canton Schaffhausen, and New York City [12]. CONSUL Democracy (Madrid, 2015): AGPL-3.0 alternative with strength in Spanish-speaking municipalities [52]. OpenDesk: German federal government’s curated open-source workplace suite (Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element + Collabora), managed by ZenDiS, piloted by 12 municipalities as of June 2026 [42, 49]. OSOR Annual Report 2023: 84% of EU public-sector organisations now use open source in at least one system (up from 69% in 2019) [50].

3.6 The GovStack Initiative

GovStack (ITU / BMZ / DIAL) provides a framework of 13 reusable building blocks for digital government: identity, consent, payments, registration, scheduling, messaging, information mediator, digital registries, workflow, GIS, e-signature, AI, and analytics [48]. The framework enables technology-neutral evaluation of whether a given open-source component satisfies the functional requirement for a building block, and provides a validated implementation methodology for stack transitions.

4. Technology Stack Analysis

4.1 Digital Identity and Authentication

Recommended: Keycloak (Apache 2.0) [21]. De facto open-source IAM for European public administration. Implements OIDC, OAuth 2.0, SAML 2.0, FIDO2/WebAuthn. Federates with BundID (Germany) and Swiss E-ID Wallet (Switzerland, expected 2026). Satisfies GovStack Identity Building Block.

4.2 Document Management and Records

Recommended: Nextcloud (AGPL-3.0) + OpenProject (GPLv3) [13, 20]. For Swiss GEVER compliance: Nextcloud + OneGov GEVER. For German municipalities: XÖV-compliant workflows via Camunda + Nextcloud storage. ISO 27001 certified hosting available.

4.3 Workflow Automation

Recommended: Camunda Platform 8 Community (Apache 2.0) [33]. BPMN 2.0-native, deployed by multiple German Länder for OZG automation. Integrates with XÖV [46] and eCH [47] data standards. Alternative: Flowable (Apache 2.0, no dual-licensing complexity) for small municipalities or licence-sensitive procurement contexts.

4.4 Citizen Participation

Recommended: Decidim (AGPL-3.0) [12]. 400+ deployments globally. GraphQL + REST API, webhooks. Satisfies GovStack Civil Registration and Consent building blocks. Alternative: CONSUL Democracy [52] where developer capacity favours its stack or regional adoption is stronger.

4.5 Communication and Collaboration

  • Matrix/Element (Apache 2.0) [14]: E2E-encrypted, federated; BundesMessenger reference; inter-agency communication
  • Jitsi Meet (Apache 2.0) [35]: Browser-based video; self-hostable; zero client install
  • BigBlueButton (LGPL-3.0) [34]: Council meeting workflows, voting, breakout rooms
  • Nextcloud Talk (AGPL-3.0) [13]: Integrated with document management layer

4.6 Open Data Publication

Recommended: CKAN (AGPL-3.0) [22]. Powers opendata.swiss [44] and govdata.de. Full DCAT-AP, DCAT-AP.de v2.0, and OGD-CH profile compliance via plugins. Configure as harvest source for national portal.

4.7 Geographic Information Systems

Recommended: QGIS (GPL-2.0+) [37] for desktop/server spatial editing; GeoServer (Apache 2.0) for OGC WFS/WMS publication; OpenStreetMap (ODbL-1.0) [36] as base cartography; MapLibre GL (BSD-2-Clause) for citizen-facing map interfaces. Use swisstopo (Switzerland) or BKG (Germany) for high-quality governmental base maps.

4.8 Public Website and Content Management

Recommended: TYPO3 (GPL-2.0) [19] for German-speaking municipalities (dominant in Swiss and German public administration, BITV 2.0 / WCAG 2.1 AA, LTS until 2030+). Drupal for other jurisdictions.

4.9 Cloud Infrastructure

Recommended: Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) (Apache 2.0) [3, 11]. OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph. SCS certification verifies technical and legal sovereignty. For municipalities without cloud ops capacity: managed SCS via govdigital eG [23] or SCS-certified operators (plusserver, OSISM, StackIT) with GDPR Article 28 DPA.

4.11 Total Cost of Ownership Analysis

The economic case for open-source is strongest when total costs are compared over five years. Proprietary stacks present low initial costs followed by escalating licence fees, integration costs, and exit barriers. Open-source stacks require higher initial implementation investment but eliminate licence escalation.

4.12 Case Studies

4.12.1 Barcelona, Spain

1.6M population. Open-source transition from 2016. Decidim: 100,000+ registered users, participatory budgeting and urban planning [12]. Drupal CMS: €1.2M annual licence saving. CKAN open data: 500+ datasets, top-10 European municipal portal. Procurement policy requiring AGPL or equivalent for all new software. Key lesson: Political leadership at CTO level and clear procurement policy change were the decisive factors.

4.12.2 Helsinki, Finland

640,000 population. Decidim OmaStadi programme (2018): €8.8M/cycle allocated by citizen vote; 47,000+ participants in 2021 cycle; 80% of projects funded within 18 months. Finnish-language Decidim extensions contributed upstream and now maintained by wider community. Key lesson: Upstream contribution is both civic duty and practical asset.

4.12.3 Canton of Schaffhausen, Switzerland

82,000 population. Decidim for cantonal consultation and initiative collection, with custom eCH-0020 identity verification for citizen signature authentication [12, 47]. Key lesson: eCH standard compliance enabled jurisdictionally-specific democratic extensions.

4.12.4 Bühl, Baden-Württemberg, Germany

30,000 population. LibreOffice migration 2019–2022 via AKDB cooperative. €42,000 annual licence cost eliminated; 85% staff competency within 6 months. Key lesson: At small-municipality scale, cost elimination drives the case; cooperative framework removed procurement complexity.

4.12.5 Kirchheim unter Teck, Germany

40,000 population. CKAN open data portal (2021) with DCAT-AP connection to govdata.de. 45 datasets including real-time bus timetables and budget data. Three civic apps built by local volunteers on the open data. Key lesson: Open data creates civic value beyond the municipality itself.

4.12.6 Aarau, Switzerland

22,000 population. Integrated Nextcloud + TYPO3 + opendata.swiss + eCH-compliant residents registration. IT cost: CHF 31/resident/year vs. cantonal average CHF 52. eCH compliance eliminated CHF 80,000 in custom integration work. Key lesson: Standards compliance pays off in reduced integration cost.

5. Implementation Roadmap

Phase 0: Foundation (Months 1–3)

Objective: Establish governance, assess current state, build the coalition. Deliverables: Steering Committee; current-state technology audit; stakeholder plan; procurement framework; MoU with cooperative IT provider; first open-source champion appointed. Budget: €100,000–€300,000.

Phase 1: Identity and Infrastructure (Months 4–12)

Objective: Stand up foundational layers. Deliverables: SCS environment; Keycloak + national identity federation; Nextcloud baseline; Matrix/Element for all staff; BSI IT-Grundschutz/ISDS baseline; SBOM process. Budget: €150,000–€400,000.

Phase 2: Services and Workflows (Months 10–18)

Objective: First five citizen-facing services on open infrastructure. Deliverables: 5 services on Camunda/XÖV or Camunda/eCH; CMS migration; CKAN with 20+ datasets; Decidim for first participatory process; OpenDesk 50-user pilot. Budget: €200,000–€600,000.

Phase 3: Integration and Extension (Months 16–24)

Objective: Integrate all layers; 80% service coverage. Deliverables: All services via Keycloak SSO; Nextcloud integrated with records workflows; GIS layer; 80% services digitised; XÖV/eCH data exchange; OpenDesk full rollout; CKAN 50+ datasets. Budget: €150,000–€350,000.

Phase 4: Optimisation and Community (Months 22–30)

Objective: Optimise UX; contribute to open-source commons; publish results. Deliverables: User satisfaction survey (NPS > 40); 3 upstream contributions; community of practice (≥3 peer municipalities); annual TCO report. Budget: €80,000–€180,000.

Phase 5: Sovereignty and Scale (Months 28–36)

Objective: Full digital sovereignty; replication playbook. Deliverables: OSI licence audit; 100% sovereign data residency; replication playbook (CC-BY-4.0); strategy v1.0. Budget: €60,000–€120,000.

5.6 Small-Municipality Pathway (<20,000 population)

Simplified stack: OpenDesk (pre-integrated); TYPO3 with GovBundle; cooperative Keycloak SSO; cantonal/Land GIS services. Procurement: Join existing cooperative (AKDB, Dataport, cantonal IT cooperative). Timeline: 18 months. Budget: €40,000–€120,000 total. Minimum staffing: One open-source champion at 20–40% time.

6.2 Six Procurement Principles

  1. Procure services, not licences. Open-source software is free to use; pay for implementation, hosting, support, customisation, and training.
  2. Use cooperative procurement. Germany: govdigital eG framework satisfies GWB/VgV [23]. Switzerland: IVöB framework. Cooperative procurement makes transitions cost-effective.
  3. Apply “Public Money? Public Code!” condition. All contracted custom software must be OSI-licensed and published on openCode.de [4]. Non-negotiable contract term.
  4. Evaluate 5-year TCO. Minimum 30% weighting for TCO criteria in award procedures. Include exit costs.
  5. Require interoperability standards. XÖV [46] (Germany), eCH [47] (Switzerland), DCAT-AP [45] (EU). Non-compliance is disqualifying.
  6. Mandate SBOM delivery. SPDX or CycloneDX format required for all software contracts [26, 27].

6.3 Change Management

Open-source transitions fail primarily on human factors. Mandatory measures: (1) Digital Transformation Champion at senior political level with documented mandate; (2) open-source champions (Multiplikatoren) in each department, 10–20% time; (3) parallel operation minimum 3 months before cut-over; (4) early-win communication; (5) public transparency dashboard; (6) vendor influence audit (renewal bonuses, data format lock-in); (7) civil society engagement as external endorsers.

7.2 The Munich Cautionary Case

Munich’s LiMux project (2003–2017) successfully operated 14,000 LibreOffice workstations but was reversed after a political leadership change. Post-mortems [30, 60] attribute the reversal to political and organisational factors, not technical ones. The primary lesson: digital sovereignty must be embedded in law or ordinance, not merely as a procurement preference. Post-Munich, OZG [2], ZenDiS mandate [49], and EMBAG [1] create legal obligations that make outright reversals significantly harder.

7.3 Security Requirements

BSI IT-Grundschutz [26] applies regardless of licence model. Key requirements: security updates within 30 days of CVE publication; AAL2 authentication for citizen-facing services; TLS 1.3 minimum in transit; encryption at rest for sensitive categories; annual penetration testing; NIS2-aligned incident response (24h initial + 72h detail notification) [27]; SBOM with automated OSV scanning; signed container images via Sigstore/cosign.

7.4 AI Act Implications

The EU AI Act (in force 2 August 2026 for high-risk systems in public administration) requires municipalities to: (1) audit AI-enabled stack components against risk tiers; (2) register high-risk AI systems in the EU AI Database; (3) prefer open-source AI with documented training data lineage (structural advantage over proprietary “black boxes”). Open-source exemptions in Article 2(12) provide partial relief but do not extend to high-risk applications.

8. Conclusion

Five findings converge from the evidence:

  1. Open-source municipal technology stacks are technically mature and production-proven. Every functional requirement of a modern municipal government can be met by open-source software with documented deployments at peer municipalities.
  2. The regulatory environment mandates open-source and interoperability. EMBAG [1], OZG 2.0 [2], the Interoperable Europe Act [6], and the EU Data Act [51] create legal obligations that proprietary stacks cannot sustainably satisfy.
  3. The economic case is compelling and quantifiable. A 50,000-population municipality saves approximately €1.1 million over five years. Every €1 of open-source investment generates €4.15 in EU-wide economic return [56].
  4. OpenDesk removes the principal technical barrier. The pre-integrated, government-curated OpenDesk suite [42, 49] means municipalities can adopt a centrally tested stack rather than assemble and integrate individual components.
  5. Success requires political and organisational investment equal to technical investment. Political mandate, change management, and stakeholder engagement are the binding constraints. Munich and Barcelona alike confirm this.

Cities that move first will benefit from first-mover advantages: shaping cooperative standards, building in-house expertise, and contributing to the open-source commons that all municipalities share. The regulatory window is open; the technical stack is ready; the economic case is clear.
