Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive, citation-complete strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on Switzerland’s EMBAG Act (2024), Germany’s OZG 2.0, the EU’s Interoperable Europe Act (2024) and Data Governance Act (2022), and case studies from Barcelona, Helsinki, Schleswig-Holstein, and the Swiss Federal Administration, we derive a 36-month phased implementation roadmap with phase-gated checklists, a procurement framework, a TCO methodology, and a KPI framework. We evaluate 14 technology components across 7 layers. We conclude that open-source municipal stacks are technically mature, regulatory-advantaged, economically superior when TCO is correctly modelled, and that success depends principally on political mandate and change management.

Keywords: digital sovereignty, open source government, GovTech, municipal digital transformation, interoperability, OZG, EMBAG, Sovereign Cloud Stack, e-government, EU Data Act, GovStack, total cost of ownership

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; fiscal pressures require sustainable technology investments. Yet most city governments remain trapped in cycles of proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede good governance [1, 29].

A different path is proven. Switzerland’s EMBAG mandates open-source release of publicly funded software [1]. Germany’s OpenDesk demonstrates a curated open-source workplace suite at scale [42]. The Sovereign Cloud Stack provides auditable, self-hostable European cloud infrastructure [3]. Schleswig-Holstein is migrating 25,000 workstations to LibreOffice [47]. The EU’s Interoperable Europe Act and Data Governance Act create binding obligations that proprietary systems cannot sustainably meet [6, 48].

2. Methodology

Multi-method research design: systematic literature review (2010–2026); comparative policy analysis (Switzerland, Germany, EU); technology evaluation matrix (7 criteria, max 35 points); Power–Interest stakeholder analysis; five case studies (Barcelona, OpenDesk/Germany, Schleswig-Holstein, Helsinki, Swiss Federal Administration). Literature review is self-improving via versioned source registry and quarterly update scripts.

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations

E-government literature covers four generations [30]: digitisation (1995–2005) [29]; online services (2005–2012) [7]; open data and participation (2012–2020) [8]; platform government and sovereignty (2020–present) [45]. Wirtz and Weyerer’s maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, transparency [7]. The GovStack initiative (ITU/DIAL) operationalises fourth-generation digital government through modular, interoperable building blocks [50].

3.2 Digital Sovereignty

EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 mandates “sharing and reuse” [5]. Interoperable Europe Act 2024 creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. Germany’s SCS (OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph) is the most concrete realisation in cloud infrastructure [3, 11]. Switzerland’s EMBAG (in force 2024-01-01) is the most progressive open-source statute globally [1].

3.3 German OZG Ecosystem

OZG 2.0 introduces Once Only, EfA, BundID, FITKO [9, 10]. Schleswig-Holstein (2021): 25,000 workstations → LibreOffice, Nextcloud, Thunderbird [47]. ZenDiS (est. 2022) manages OpenDesk; 2023 annual report: 10,000+ federal civil servants on OpenDesk [49].

3.4 Swiss Digital Services

eCH standards [51]; Swiss eID-Gesetz 2023 [52]; opendata.swiss [44]; GEVER [43]. E-Government Strategy 2024–2027 mandates open standards [16].

3.5 Open-Source Communities

Decidim: 400+ deployments, Barcelona’s 70,000-participant budget [12, 54]. CONSUL Democracy: direct-democracy alternative [53]. Matrix/Element: BundesMessenger reference [14]. Nextcloud: ~40,000 Swiss federal civil servants [13]. OpenDesk: first government-curated suite at scale [42].

3.6 EU Data Governance Act and Data Act

DGA (EU 2022/868, applicable September 2023): non-exclusive public-sector data sharing [48]. Data Act (EU 2023/2854, applicable September 2025): cloud switching rights, data portability, government emergency access [55]. Together they structurally advantage open-source architectures.

3.7 GovStack

ITU/DIAL (2021): building blocks (identity, payments, registration, messaging, workflow) with open specifications, mapping directly to the technology stack in Section 4 [50].

3.8 Case Studies

Barcelona: 70% open-source spending target; Decidim developed and open-sourced; 70,000+ in first participatory budget [54]. Schleswig-Holstein: 25,000 workstations, phased 2023–2026 [47]. Helsinki: 1,200+ datasets, Decidim, open-source-first procurement [56]. Swiss Federal: ~40,000 civil servants on Nextcloud, integrated with eID and GEVER [13].

4. Technology Stack Analysis

4.1 Digital Identity and Authentication

Recommended: Keycloak [21] — Apache 2.0; OIDC, OAuth 2.0, SAML 2.0, FIDO2; federates with BundID and Swiss eID. Composite score: 34/35.

4.2 Document Management and Records

Recommended: Nextcloud + Alfresco Community [13] — Alfresco provides ISO 15489-compliant records management; for Swiss GEVER: CMI GEVER or Fabasoft over Nextcloud. Composite: 34/35.

4.3 Workflow Automation

Recommended: Camunda Platform 8 Community or Flowable [33] — BPMN 2.0 native; XÖV/eCH integration. Composite: 29/35.

4.4 Citizen Participation

Recommended: Decidim [12] (AGPL-3.0; 33/35). Alternative: CONSUL Democracy [53].

4.8 CMS

TYPO3 (German-speaking public sector) [19] · Drupal (international). Both: WCAG 2.1 AA / BITV 2.0 compliant.

4.9 Cloud Infrastructure

Recommended: Sovereign Cloud Stack (SCS) [3, 11] — OpenStack + Kubernetes + Ceph; certified hosters: plusserver, OSISM, REGIO iT; govdigital eG operates shared SCS cloud [23]. Score: 32/35.

4.10 Office Productivity

Recommended: LibreOffice + Collabora Online — MPL-2.0; ODF native; 200M+ users; Schleswig-Holstein reference deployment. Score: 34/35.

4.11 Web Analytics

Recommended: Matomo — GPL-3.0; self-hosted; GDPR-compliant; required under DSK ruling (Google Analytics impermissible for German public authorities) [57]. Score: 32/35.

4.12 DevOps

Recommended: Forgejo + Woodpecker CI or GitLab CE. Forgejo: MIT; Woodpecker: Apache 2.0. Score: 29/35.

4.13 ERP

Maintain existing ERP through Phase 1–3; implement open interfaces (XÖV/eCH). Plan open-source ERP (ERPNext Community, Odoo Community) as Phase 4+ initiative.

6. Stakeholder and Procurement Strategy

6.3 RFP Minimum Requirements

1.  LICENCE: OSI-approved open-source licence for all components.
2.  SOURCE CODE: Published on openCode.de within 30 days of acceptance.
3.  DATA PORTABILITY: Export in ODF/CSV/JSON/XML/DCAT-AP within 24h.
4.  API: Documented REST or GraphQL. No proprietary integration formats.
5.  AUTHENTICATION: Keycloak/OIDC. No proprietary identity silos.
6.  SECURITY: BSI IT-Grundschutz (DE) / ISDS (CH). Pentest at acceptance.
7.  ACCESSIBILITY: WCAG 2.1 AA / BITV 2.0. Accessibility statement.
8.  DATA RESIDENCY: EU/EEA; not subject to non-EU data access laws.
9.  INTEROPERABILITY: XÖV (DE) / eCH (CH) for data exchange interfaces.
10. EXIT PLAN: Full data migration within 90 days at no additional cost.

Munich LiMux warning: The 2003–2017 migration was reversed due to political leadership change, not technical failure. 14 years of operation proved technical viability. Political risk management is as important as technical planning [30].

8. Conclusion

(1) Technical maturity: Every municipal requirement is met by production-proven open-source software.

(2) Regulatory mandate: EMBAG, OZG 2.0, Interoperable Europe Act, DGA, and Data Act structurally advantage open-source.

(3) Economic case: 5-year TCO systematically favours open-source when vendor lock-in risk is correctly valued. Licence savings: typically €100–300 per user per year.

(4) Proven at all scales: Barcelona (1.6M), Helsinki (650K), Swiss cantons, Schleswig-Holstein (25K workstations).

(5) Governance is the binding constraint. The technology is ready. Three immediate actions: (1) pass a council resolution; (2) begin Keycloak and Nextcloud; (3) join govdigital eG. All initiable simultaneously.
