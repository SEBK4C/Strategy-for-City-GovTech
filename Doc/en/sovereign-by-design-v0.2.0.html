§0. Executive Stakeholder Guide

For City Administrators and Senior Managers

Open-source municipal technology is no longer an experiment — it is mandated by law in Switzerland (EMBAG) and Germany (OZG 2.0). The fiscal case is compelling: proprietary productivity suites cost €100–300 per seat per year in licence fees alone; the open-source equivalent costs primarily labour, spread across cooperative procurement. The most frequently underestimated risk is political, not technical. Recommended reading: §5 and §6.

For Elected Officials

Digital sovereignty is a democratic value, not a technical preference. When software running a city’s administration is privately owned, that vendor can withdraw support, raise prices, or be acquired — with no democratic recourse. Switzerland’s parliament enacted EMBAG in 2023 precisely because this principle resonates across the political spectrum. Recommended reading: §1, §3.2, §8.

For Public-Sector IT Teams

Every component evaluated here has production deployments at peer municipalities. The most important decisions: (1) start with Keycloak IAM; (2) choose an SCS-certified host before deploying application layers; (3) insist on XÖV (Germany) or eCH (Switzerland) standards compliance in every procurement. Recommended reading: §4, §5, §7.3.

For Procurement Offices

You are buying services (implementation, hosting, support, training), not rights to use software. govdigital eG framework agreements satisfy German GWB §97ff.; Swiss cantonal cooperatives cover BöB requirements. All custom-development contracts must include: software released under an OSI licence and published on openCode.de. Evaluate five-year TCO, not headline implementation cost (§4.11). Recommended reading: §6.2, §4.11.

For Civil-Society Groups and Open-Source Communities

This strategy positions civic communities as co-producers. Decidim deployments actively solicit participation in budget allocation. openCode.de pools contributions so improvements made by one municipality benefit all. Contributions to upstream projects (Decidim, Nextcloud, CKAN, Matrix) directly improve the commons. Recommended reading: §3.5, §6.1.

For Sovereign Technology Providers

This strategy creates substantial market opportunity for providers delivering implementation, hosting, support, and training — on the explicit condition of data sovereignty and open-source compliance. SCS certification or govdigital eG membership unlocks framework contract access. Contributing to openCode.de and upstream communities is a reputational advantage in this market. Recommended reading: §4, §6.

Abstract

Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on exemplary practices from the Swiss federal administration’s EMBAG legislation, Germany’s OZG reform programme and Sovereign Cloud Stack initiative, the ZenDiS-managed OpenDesk platform, and the wider European sovereign technology community — including GovStack, eCH, Decidim, CONSUL Democracy, and Matrix — we derive a first-principles implementation roadmap, stakeholder engagement framework, and procurement strategy. Case studies from small and mid-size municipalities in Switzerland and Germany demonstrate that the transition is achievable across the full range of city sizes. We conclude that open-source municipal technology stacks are not merely technically viable but fiscally superior, democratically necessary, and legally mandated in an increasing number of jurisdictions.

Keywords: digital sovereignty, open source government, GovTech, municipal digital transformation, OZG, EMBAG, Sovereign Cloud Stack, ZenDiS, eCH, GovStack

§1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; fiscal pressures require sustainable, cost-effective technology investments. Yet most city governments worldwide remain trapped in proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems [1, 29].

Switzerland’s 2023 EMBAG legislation mandates that federal software developed with public funds be released as open source by default [1]. Germany’s OZG reform programme, Sovereign Cloud Stack, and OpenDesk — managed by ZenDiS GmbH — represent the most ambitious open-source government technology programme in Europe [2, 3, 42, 47]. The GovStack initiative (ITU/GIZ/DIAL) provides an internationally standardised building-block framework directly applicable to European municipal implementations [50].

1.1 Scope and Definitions

Digital sovereignty is the ability of a government to make independent decisions about its digital infrastructure — including the right to inspect, modify, audit, and migrate software without dependency on a single vendor [3, 47].

1.2 Research Questions

  1. What does a state-of-the-art open-source municipal technology stack look like in 2026?
  2. What lessons can be drawn from the Swiss, German, and European sovereign technology communities?
  3. What is the optimal phased implementation pathway across the full population range?
  4. How should procurement, stakeholder engagement, and risk management be structured?

§2. Methodology

Multi-method research design: systematic literature review (2010–2026); comparative policy analysis (Switzerland, Germany, EU); technology stack evaluation via structured scoring matrix; stakeholder analysis; case study analysis of municipalities across the population range.

§3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations

E-government has evolved through four phases [30]. Lathrop and Ruma (2010) established the three pillars of open government: transparency, participation, and collaboration [53]. Wirtz and Weyerer’s maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7].

3.2 Digital Sovereignty

The Interoperable Europe Act (Regulation (EU) 2024/903, CELEX 32024R0903) creates binding interoperability obligations [6]. The EU Data Act (2023/2854, applicable September 2025) adds harmonised data-sharing obligations [52]. ZenDiS GmbH, established by the German federal government in 2022, manages OpenDesk and coordinates sovereign technology development [47]. OSOR (EU Open Source Observatory) tracks adoption across member states annually [54]. Switzerland’s EMBAG (in force January 2024) makes it one of the most progressive open-source mandating jurisdictions globally [1].

3.3 The German OZG Ecosystem

OZG 2.0 (2024): “Once Only” principle, EfA (Einer für Alle) approach, BundID and FITKO [2, 9]. The Deutsche Verwaltungscloud-Strategie (DVS) positions SCS as the target architecture [56]. openCode.de provides a centralised government open-source repository [10].

3.4 Swiss Digital Services

Key Swiss digital infrastructure: Fedlex (federal law, open standards) [1]; opendata.swiss (CKAN-based national OGD portal) [44]; GEVER (records management, BAR) [43]; Swiss E-ID/BGEID (W3C Verifiable Credentials, SWIYU wallet, entering service 2026) [51]; eCH standards (ech.ch — eCH-0160 records, eCH-0039 data exchange, eCH-0261 E-ID; Swiss equivalent of XÖV) [48].

3.5 Sovereign Technology Communities

ZenDiS GmbH [47] — central coordination for digital sovereignty in German public administration, manages OpenDesk. Decidim [12] — 400+ deployments, AGPL-3.0. CONSUL Democracy [49] — 200+ cities, GPL-3.0, direct democracy tools. GovStack [50] — 9 building blocks (identity, payments, consent, workflow, GIS…). Matrix/Element [14] — BundesMessenger, Tchap. Nextcloud [13] — Swiss federal and German Länder deployments. OpenDesk [42, 47] — curated government suite (Nextcloud + Cryptpad + OpenProject + Jitsi + Element).

3.7 Small and Mid-Size Municipality Case Studies

Canton of Schaffhausen (~83,000): Decidim deployed 2020–2021 for cantonal participatory democracy; self-hosted; contributed translations upstream [12].

AKDB cooperative municipalities, Bavaria: Open-source document management and citizen portals via BayernPortal framework; shared procurement and operations model [24].

Bodenseekreis, Baden-Württemberg (~214,000, 23 municipalities): District-coordinated TYPO3 rollout with shared hosting and common design system [19].

Lausanne, Switzerland (~145,000): CKAN open data portal, DCAT-AP harvesting to opendata.swiss, eCH-compliant metadata [44, 48].

EfA pilot municipalities, Germany: 100+ EfA services on openCode.de by 2025; a municipality of 10,000 can deploy the same service as a city of 500,000 at a fraction of the development cost [10, 56].

§5. Implementation Roadmap

Phase 0: Foundation (Months 1–3)

Steering committee; current-state audit; stakeholder plan; procurement framework; MoU with cooperative IT provider; GovStack building-block mapping [50]. Budget: €200k–500k for Phase 0–1.

Phase 1: Identity and Infrastructure (Months 4–12)

SCS environment; Keycloak federated with BundID/BGEID; Nextcloud; Matrix/Element; BSI IT-Grundschutz / ISDS baseline. Success: 100% IT staff via Keycloak SSO; storage migrated to Nextcloud.

Phase 2: Services and Workflows (Months 10–18)

Five highest-volume services on Camunda/XÖV (DE) or Camunda/eCH (CH); CMS migration; CKAN with DCAT-AP; Decidim or CONSUL. Success: 80% of target service volume in new system.

Phase 3: Integration and Extension (Months 16–24)

Keycloak SSO across all services; GIS layer; 80% services digitised; XÖV/eCH data exchange; EU Data Act compliance mapped [52]. Success: 80% paperless end-to-end.

Phase 4: Optimisation and Community (Months 22–30)

NPS survey (target >40); first upstream contributions; community of practice; TCO report. Success: ≥3 upstream improvements; ≥3 peer municipalities engaged.

Phase 5: Sovereignty and Scale (Months 28–36)

Licence compliance audit; sovereign data residency verified; replication playbook; strategy paper v1.0. Success: zero proprietary single-vendor dependencies in critical path.

§6. Stakeholder and Procurement Strategy

6.2 Procurement Framework (key principles)

  1. Procure services, not licences. Implementation, hosting, support, training.
  2. Use cooperative procurement. govdigital eG (Germany, GWB §97ff.); cantonal cooperatives (Switzerland, BöB SR 172.056.1) [23].
  3. Public Money? Public Code! All custom software released under OSI licence on openCode.de [4].
  4. Evaluate five-year TCO using the framework in §4.11.
  5. Require interoperability standards: XÖV (DE) [46], eCH (CH) [48], DCAT-AP (EU).
  6. Prefer SCS-certified or govdigital eG providers [3, 23].
  7. Reference GovStack building-block interfaces [50] to avoid specification lock-in.

7.2 The Munich Cautionary Case

The Munich LiMux project (2003–2017) achieved documented cost savings during operation. Reversal was driven by political leadership change, inadequate change management, and compatibility issues — not technical failure [30]. The reversal cost an estimated €50M. This asymmetry strengthens the long-run economic case.

7.4 Regulatory Compliance Framework

GDPR/nDSG: Self-hosted infrastructure with explicit data retention policies satisfies data minimisation; open-source enables independent design audit. Interoperable Europe Act [6]: Open APIs, XÖV/eCH, DCAT-AP satisfy core obligations. EU Data Act [52]: CKAN with DCAT-AP is the reference implementation. NIS2 [27]: BSI IT-Grundschutz satisfies German transposition; Swiss ISDS equivalent. AI Act [57]: Citizen-facing decision AI is “high-risk” (Annex III); open-source enables mandatory conformity audit. EMBAG/OZG 2.0: Create positive legal obligations for open-source-first procurement.

§8. Conclusion

1. Technically mature and production-proven. Every functional requirement of a modern municipal government can be met by open-source software with documented deployments from Schaffhausen (~83,000) to Barcelona (1.6M).

2. Regulatory mandated. EMBAG, OZG 2.0, Interoperable Europe Act, EU Data Act, and NIS2 create cumulative legal obligations that proprietary, vendor-locked systems face growing difficulty satisfying across five simultaneous frameworks.

3. Fiscally superior at total cost. ~18% lower five-year TCO for a 500-staff municipality. The exit cost asymmetry (Munich: ~€50M reversal) makes open-source the conservative fiscal choice over a 10-year horizon. Cooperative procurement reduces per-municipality costs by 30–50%.

4. Success is primarily political and organisational. Clear mandate, skilled change management, and sustained stakeholder engagement are the binding constraints. The GovStack building-block framework, ZenDiS-managed OpenDesk, and the EfA service library substantially reduce the technical barrier for small municipalities.
