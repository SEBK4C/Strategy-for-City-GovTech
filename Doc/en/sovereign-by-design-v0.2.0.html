Abstract. Municipal governments are the closest layer of democratic administration to citizens, yet they face an acute structural tension: the digital tools they depend on are increasingly proprietary, vendor-locked, and misaligned with public-interest values. This paper presents a comprehensive strategy for implementing a state-of-the-art, open-source governmental administration technology stack in city governments. Drawing on Swiss EMBAG legislation, Germany’s OZG reform, Sovereign Cloud Stack, OpenDesk/ZenDiS, and the wider European sovereign technology community, we provide a first-principles implementation roadmap, procurement strategy, stakeholder framework, and total cost of ownership analysis. Case studies cover Barcelona, Helsinki, Canton Schaffhausen, and the OpenDesk programme. A five-year TCO analysis shows a 35% saving (€2.1M for the 800-staff reference municipality), rising to 55–65% in cooperative arrangements. The paper is addressed to all municipal digital transformation stakeholders and is designed to improve on a recurring quarterly basis.

1. Introduction

The digital transformation of municipal government is no longer optional. Citizens expect seamless, secure, and accessible digital services; regulators demand interoperability and data protection; and fiscal pressures require sustainable technology investments. Yet the majority of city governments remain trapped in proprietary vendor dependency, expensive licence agreements, and fragmented systems that impede good governance [1, 29].

Switzerland’s EMBAG (2024) mandates open-source release of publicly funded software. Germany’s OZG 2.0, Sovereign Cloud Stack, and OpenDesk represent Europe’s most ambitious open-source government technology programme [2, 3, 42]. The 2024 Interoperable Europe Act, EU AI Act, and NIS2 Directive collectively create legal obligations that proprietary systems cannot sustainably satisfy [6, 27, 52].

1.1 Research Questions

  1. What does a state-of-the-art open-source municipal technology stack look like in 2026?
  2. What lessons can be drawn from Swiss, German, and European case studies?

  4. How should procurement, stakeholder engagement, and risk management be structured?

3. Literature Review

3.1 Theoretical Foundations

E-government has evolved through four phases [30]: digitisation (1995–2005), online service delivery (2005–2012), open data and participation (2012–2020), and the current platform/sovereignty turn (2020–present). Wirtz & Weyerer’s maturity model identifies five dimensions: technical infrastructure, process quality, service quality, citizen orientation, and transparency [7]. Lathrop & Ruma (2010) establish open government as a design objective operationalised in OECD principles and European Open Government Partnership action plans [50].

3.2 Digital Sovereignty

The EU Open Source Strategy 2020–2023 mandates preference for open source in EU procurement [5]. The 2024 Interoperable Europe Act creates binding cross-border interoperability obligations [6]. Germany’s SCS is the most concrete realisation of cloud sovereignty [3]. Switzerland’s EMBAG places it among the most progressive open-source mandating jurisdictions globally [1]. ZenDiS GmbH operationalises digital sovereignty at federal level through the OpenDesk suite [42, 56].

3.3 German OZG Ecosystem

OZG 2.0 (2024) introduces the “Once Only” principle, EfA approach, BundID, and FITKO coordination framework [2, 9]. openCode.de hosts 400+ government open-source repositories [10]. Dataport AöR and AKDB demonstrate the cooperative IT delivery model [24, 58].

3.4 Swiss Federal and Cantonal Digital Services

Key infrastructure: Fedlex (federal law platform), opendata.swiss (CKAN-based national OGD portal), GEVER (records management), Swiss E-ID (post-2021 decentralised identity) [1, 43, 44, 51]. The E-Government Strategy 2024–2027 mandates open standards and interoperability [16].

3.5 eCH Standards

eCH is Switzerland’s e-government standards body, publishing XML-based formats (eCH-0010, eCH-0044, eCH-0058, eCH-0160, eCH-0200) under CC0 [47]. eCH standards are mandatory for cantonal systems and align with DCAT-AP at the open data layer. Any open-source stack in a Swiss municipality must implement eCH or provide certified connectors.

3.6 GovStack Building Blocks

ITU/DIAL’s GovStack (2021) provides modular functional specifications (identity, payments, consent, messaging, registries) compatible with the European Interoperability Framework [6, 48]. Open-source implementations include Keycloak (identity) and CKAN (data publishing) [21, 22]. Municipalities adopting GovStack-compliant implementations can participate in cross-border service delivery under the Interoperable Europe Act.

3.7 GAIA-X and Data Spaces

GAIA-X establishes principles and a Trust Framework for federated European data infrastructure [59]. SCS is GAIA-X compliant, enabling municipalities to participate in EU data spaces (urban mobility, smart cities) without sacrificing sovereignty [3, 11, 60].

3.8 EU AI Act

The EU AI Act (2024) classifies AI in traffic management, benefits assessment, urban planning, and law enforcement automation as high-risk, requiring conformity assessment, technical documentation, and human oversight [52]. Social scoring by public authorities is prohibited. Open-source AI deployments benefit from reduced compliance burden due to transparency obligations being met by published source code and model cards.

3.9 Case Studies

Barcelona

Barcelona’s 2016–2020 Digital City Plan pioneered open-source participatory democracy via Decidim, with 40,000+ citizens participating in €75M of participatory budgeting decisions [55, 61]. The Decidim Association, established to ensure platform governance transcends any single city, is a governance model for open-source sovereignty.

Helsinki

Helsinki’s Digital Strategy 2021–2025 released the Helsinki Design System as open-source shared infrastructure, adopted by 30+ Finnish municipalities [57]. The hri.fi open data portal publishes 1,200+ datasets under CC-BY / CC0 on CKAN with DCAT-AP metadata [22, 57].

Canton Schaffhausen

The first Swiss-German-language public administration to deploy Decidim (2021), for cantonal public consultation, complementing Switzerland’s direct-democracy tradition [12].

OpenDesk (Germany)

ZenDiS’s OpenDesk integrates Nextcloud, Cryptpad, OpenProject, Jitsi, Element, and Collabora into a government-validated containerised workplace suite, deployed in federal ministries and piloted in Länder administrations. Full deployment code published on openCode.de [42, 56].

Munich LiMux (Cautionary Case)

The 2017 reversal of Munich’s 14-year open-source transition was driven by political change, inadequate change management, and compatibility friction — not technical failure. Implications: embed digital sovereignty in legislation, not only procurement policy; invest equally in organisational change and technology [62].

6. Stakeholder and Procurement Strategy

Key procurement principles: procure services not licences; five-year TCO evaluation; cooperative procurement frameworks (govdigital eG, cantonal IT cooperatives); “Public Money? Public Code!” as contract condition; open standards compliance as disqualifying criterion; exit terms mandatory; EU Procurement Directive MEAT criterion alignment [4, 23, 64].

Change management: Digital Transformation Champion at political level; departmental open-source champions (20% time allocation); minimum 3-month parallel operation; public transparency dashboard; user-centred service design throughout.

7. Risk Analysis

Highest-risk items (score ≥9): political reversal, integration failure, user adoption failure, EU AI Act non-compliance. All are addressable through non-technical measures: legislation embedding, change management investment, phased decision gates, and early conformity assessment. The Munich case confirms political risk — not technical risk — is the dominant factor in open-source transitions [62].

9. Conclusion

Five convergent findings: (1) open-source stacks are technically mature and production-proven; (2) the regulatory environment has shifted decisively in their favour; (3) the five-year economic case shows 35% TCO saving, rising to 55–65% in cooperative arrangements; (4) ZenDiS, govdigital eG, and cooperative providers have dramatically reduced activation energy; (5) success requires equal investment in political mandate, change management, and technology. Every municipality that moves creates value for the entire open-source commons.
